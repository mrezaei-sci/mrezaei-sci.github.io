Effective-Connectivity Markers of Motor Skill Learning in the Cortico–Basal Ganglia Circuit fMRIsDCMMotor learning

Directional reorganization after 1‑week foot‑motor practice: ↑ SMA→M1, ↑ thalamus→striatal drive, ↑ putamen→pallidum; ↓ M1→putamen.

Abstract

Practice changes how we move and how the brain talks to itself. We asked whether a short foot-motor training program leaves a directional “signature” in resting networks. Thirteen healthy adults trained for one week to draw shapes with ankle-controlled movements. Speed rose by ~24% and distance-to-target fell by ~15%. Resting-state fMRI was acquired before and after training. Using spectral dynamic causal modeling, we estimated directed coupling within a hypothesis-driven motor circuit (supplementary motor area, primary motor cortex, dorsal premotor cortex, thalamus, caudate, putamen, pallidum, and a cerebellar relay). Group analysis revealed selective changes: stronger right SMA→left M1 influence, stronger thalamus→striatal drive (to caudate and putamen), and stronger putamen→pallidum coupling; in contrast, left M1→left putamen decreased. These shifts suggest that practice increases top-down drive from planning to execution and reinforces basal ganglia pathways that favor the trained action, while pruning less efficient cortico-striatal routes. The results provide directional evidence that short practice reorganizes cortico–basal ganglia communication at rest and align with a model in which reinforcement and error-based processes jointly shape skill. This mechanistic picture points to practical targets for rehabilitation, such as pairing practice with stimulation of SMA or strategies that enhance thalamic/striatal engagement.

Keywords— motor learning, resting-state fMRI, spectral dynamic causal modeling, effective connectivity, neurorehabilitation

Brain Network Reconfiguration During Creative Playmaking: A Task‑fMRI and tHOFC Investigation Task‑fMRItHOFCSports cognition

  • 1st Mohammad Rezaei — Department of Engineering Science, University of Tehran, Tehran, Iran; moh.rezaei@ut.ac.ir
  • 2nd Mahdi Siami — Department of Engineering Science, University of Tehran, Tehran, Iran
  • 3rd Alireza Talesh Jafadideh* — Department of Engineering Science, University of Tehran, Tehran, Iran; alireza.talesh@ut.ac.ir
Abstract

Creativity is the weapon of elite soccer players. The best players often make a surprising move, such as an unexpected passing lane or invent an unscripted dribble, while everyone else is still reading the play. Neuroimaging work has shown that years of training reshape functional brain architecture; yet, most studies rely on pairwise (low-order) connectivity and resting-state scans, leaving a significant gap: what does the brain do at the exact moment a fresh idea appears?

We wanted to see what the brain is doing right when that fresh idea pops up. Thirty-five experienced male players lay in an fMRI scanner, watched real match clips, and—at a freeze-frame—quietly pictured the most original, goal-focused move they could. We measured topographical high-order functional connectivity (tHOFC), a “correlation-of-correlations” method that tracks how whole-brain networks echo one another.

Players rated highly by expert coaches (Soccer Vision Training, SVT) didn’t just crank up every connection. Instead, six specific links from a right-side peripheral-vision area to both dorsal and ventral attention hubs got weaker, while the brain’s overall tHOFC level stayed the same. In short, creative experts seem to loosen the usual vision-attention handshake, likely freeing up mental space to run inner simulations and hatch original plays. This is the first sports-cognition study to use tHOFC during a live-play creativity task, and it hints that targeted brain training or gentle neuro-stimulation could someday help players unlock more on-field ideas.

Keywords— Creative decision-making, Functional MRI (fMRI), Topographical high-order functional connectivity (tHOFC)

Warm Cognition Framework for NPCs Affective ComputingDesign

Conceptual framework that augments cold, logic-only agents with emotion-aware appraisal, enabling natural, context-sensitive NPC behaviors. This phase covers theory and design specifications; no software release yet.

Overview
Emotions play a fundamental role in human cognition and interactions. Conventional AI systems often operate on the basis of cold cognition and cannot adapt behavior to user affect, leading to monotonous, unnatural NPC interactions. Building on affective computing, this project specifies a referenceable framework that bridges purely logical decision-making and emotion-driven decision-making for richer human–machine interaction.
